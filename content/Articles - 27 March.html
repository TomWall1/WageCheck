If you work weekends or public holidays in hospitality, there's a high chance your penalty rates have been applied incorrectly — or not applied at all. Sunday shifts are the most frequently underpaid scenario across the entire award. This page shows every rate you're entitled to and the calculator checks your actual shifts.

If you work Saturdays, Sundays, public holidays, or late nights in any hospitality venue — this applies to you.

Real example

Scenario: Casual kitchen hand, Level 1. 6-hour Sunday shift. What they were paid: $30.13/hr (standard casual rate) What should have happened: Sunday casual rate at Level 1 — $42.18/hr Underpayment: ~$73 for one shift. ~$3,800/year working one Sunday per week. Why it happens: Employer pays the same casual rate every day. The Sunday multiplier is never applied.

Evening and late-night penalties

This is one of the least understood parts of the award — and one of the biggest sources of underpayment.

The evening and late-night loadings are additions to your base rate, applied per hour. They stack on top of any weekend rate already in effect.

Mini example: You work a Saturday shift from 8pm to 2am. Hours 8pm–midnight = Saturday rate + evening loading. Hours midnight–2am = Sunday rate + late-night loading. Most employers pay Saturday rate for the whole shift. That's wrong from midnight onwards.

Most payslips don't show these separately — but they must still be paid.

Flat rate every day of the week One rate regardless of Saturday, Sunday, or public holiday. Unless the flat rate has been formally assessed against every penalty scenario, this is almost always underpayment. → [Check your pay →]

Wrong public holiday multiplier Some employers apply 2× when the award specifies 2.25×. On a full shift, that's a meaningful shortfall on every public holiday worked.

Shift crossing midnight not split correctly Saturday rates end at midnight. From midnight, Sunday rates apply. Many employers pay one rate for the whole overnight shift.

Frequently asked questions

Can I recover penalty rates I wasn't paid? Yes — up to 6 years back under the Fair Work Act. The Fair Work Ombudsman can recover these on your behalf.

Hospitality Award Casual Pay Rates 2025–26

Last updated: March 2026 · Rates effective 1 July 2025 · MA000009

If you're a casual hospitality worker, there's a high chance you've been told the 25% loading covers weekends. It doesn't. The loading and penalty rates are separate entitlements — both apply every time. This is one of the most common misrepresentations in the industry, and it costs casual workers significantly every weekend.

If you work casual shifts in any hospitality venue — this applies to you.

Real example

Scenario: Casual food and beverage attendant, Level 2. 6-hour Sunday shift. What they were paid: $31.60/hr (casual base — loading included) What should have happened: Casual Sunday rate at Level 2 — $44.24/hr Underpayment: ~$76 for one shift. ~$3,900/year on one Sunday per week. Why it happens: Employer pays the casual rate every day assuming the loading covers the penalty. It doesn't.

What is the 25% casual loading?

The casual loading compensates for entitlements you don't receive as a casual employee:

  • No paid annual leave
  • No paid personal leave
  • No notice of termination
  • No redundancy pay

It is applied to the base rate to produce your casual ordinary hourly rate. That's your starting point — not your full entitlement on weekends.

The loading does not replace penalty rates. Both apply.

Minimum engagement — 3 hours per shift

Every casual shift must be paid for a minimum of 3 hours, even if you're sent home early. If you're regularly paid for less than 3 hours on short shifts, you're owed the difference.

Casual conversion — your right after 12 months

After 12 months of regular and systematic casual work, you have the right to request conversion to permanent part-time or full-time employment.

Ask yourself: are you being rostered on a consistent pattern, trusted with regular responsibilities, and expected to be available week to week? If yes, you may already qualify.

Your employer must respond in writing and, if declining, must provide genuine operational reasons. Vague refusals can be challenged at the Fair Work Commission.

Frequently asked questions

Does the 25% loading mean I earn more than a permanent worker? On ordinary weekdays, yes. But permanent workers accrue leave entitlements and receive notice on termination — benefits that have real monetary value over time.

Can my employer remove the loading if I get other benefits? Not without a formal, legally compliant arrangement. The loading is an award entitlement and cannot be waived informally.

My roster is the same every week — am I still casual? Possibly not after 12 months. Regular and systematic work — even with varying hours — can trigger conversion rights. If your employer has never raised this after 12 months, they may be in breach.

Hospitality Award Overtime Rates 2025–26

Last updated: March 2026 · Rates effective 1 July 2025 · MA000009

If you regularly work long hours in hospitality and your pay looks identical every week, there's a high chance overtime isn't being applied. Extra hours are common in the industry. Not being paid for them is just as common. This page explains when overtime applies and what you must receive.

If you work more than 38 hours a week or more than 10 hours in a single shift — overtime applies to you.

Real example

Scenario: Full-time cook, Level 3. Works 45 hours across one week — five 9-hour days. What they were paid: $26.10/hr flat for all 45 hours What should have happened: 38 hours at $26.10/hr; 7 hours at overtime rates ($39.15/hr then $52.20/hr) Underpayment: ~$130 that week. ~$6,700/year if this happens weekly. Why it happens: Employer treats any extra hours as "part of the job." The overtime calculation is never run.

When does overtime apply?

Under the Hospitality Award, overtime is triggered by two thresholds:

  • Daily: After more than 10 hours worked in a single day
  • Weekly: After more than 38 ordinary hours in a week

Both thresholds exist independently. Exceed either one — overtime applies to the excess hours.

If your payslip never shows overtime despite regularly working past these thresholds, that's a red flag. → [Check your pay →]

Time off in lieu (TOIL)

Under some conditions, TOIL can substitute for overtime pay — but only if:

  • Agreed in writing before the overtime is worked
  • Taken at the overtime rate — 1 hour at time-and-a-half equals 1.5 hours TOIL, not 1 hour

Hour-for-hour TOIL is underpayment. If you've been taking TOIL on a 1:1 basis, the difference is owed. → [Check your pay →]

Does overtime apply to casual employees?

Daily overtime does. A casual working more than 10 hours in a day is entitled to overtime on the excess hours.

Weekly overtime in the same way as permanent employees generally does not apply to casuals under this award. But the daily threshold applies regardless of employment type.

⚠️ Common overtime underpayments

Long shifts with no overtime applied A 12-hour shift contains 2 hours of overtime under this award. Many employers pay a flat shift rate regardless of length. → [Check your pay →]

Salary that "absorbs" all hours A salary only covers overtime if it demonstrably exceeds total award obligations across every week worked. Most don't — especially during peak periods.

Part-time workers pushed beyond agreed hours Agreed hours for a part-time worker are ordinary hours. Work beyond them is overtime — even if the total week doesn't exceed 38.

TOIL taken hour-for-hour 1 hour at time-and-a-half = 1.5 hours TOIL minimum. Anything less is underpayment.

These issues rarely happen in isolation — and even one can result in hundreds or thousands in underpayments per year.

Frequently asked questions

My employer says my salary covers all hours — is that valid? Only if the salary exceeds all award obligations including overtime in every week you work. If you regularly work well beyond 38 hours, ask your employer to show you the calculation. If they can't, you may be owed the difference.

Can I claim back unpaid overtime from previous years? Yes — up to 6 years under the Fair Work Act. The Fair Work Ombudsman can investigate and recover this on your behalf.

Do supervisors and managers get overtime? Most do. The exemption only applies where a salary genuinely covers all overtime obligations. Most hospitality managers on fixed salaries are underpaid on this basis.

Hospitality Award Classification Levels 2025 — Which Level Are You?

Last updated: March 2026 · Rates effective 1 July 2025 · MA000009

If you've never been told your classification level, there's a high chance it's been set too low. Misclassification is one of the most common — and least visible — forms of underpayment in hospitality. Your employer sets the level, but the law requires them to base it on what you actually do. A level set for convenience rather than duties is underpayment.

If you've never checked your classification level under the Hospitality Award — that's the first thing to fix.

Real example

Scenario: Experienced waitstaff, 3 years in role, regularly training junior staff. Classified at Level 2. What they were paid: $31.60/hr casual What should have happened: Training and supervisory duties = Level 3 minimum. Casual rate: $32.63/hr Underpayment: ~$1.03/hr × 25hrs/week × 50 weeks = ~$1,288/year — before penalty rate differences Why it happens: Classification was set on day one and never reviewed as responsibilities grew.

What each level actually means

Level 1 — You're new to the industry or in a simple, closely supervised role. Basic tasks under direct instruction.

Level 2 — You work independently in your area once shown what to do. Routine situations handled without constant supervision.

Level 3 — You're skilled, experienced, and may train or guide newer staff. You handle complex situations and operate across multiple functions.

Level 4 — You supervise a team, hold trade qualifications, or manage a section with budget and staffing responsibility.

Level 5 — Senior management across a venue or department. Full operational and financial responsibility.

The self-check

Ask yourself: are you trusted to run shifts, train others, or solve problems independently? If yes, you may already be performing Level 3 duties regardless of what your payslip says.

Frequently asked questions

My title says 'supervisor' — does that mean Level 4? Not automatically. The level is determined by duties, not title. If your "supervisor" role involves genuine shift management and team responsibility, Level 4 may apply. A courtesy title alone doesn't move you up.

Can I be on a lower level than colleagues doing the same job? No — not legitimately. Employees performing equivalent duties should receive equivalent classification. If you're doing the same work as someone on a higher level, you're entitled to reclassification.

What if I've been misclassified for years? You can recover underpayments going back 6 years. Misclassification affects every ordinary hour and every penalty rate throughout that period. Contact the Fair Work Ombudsman on 13 13 94.

What is a modern award?

A modern award is a legal document set by the Fair Work Commission that establishes minimum pay rates and employment conditions for a specific industry or occupation.

It covers:

  • Minimum hourly rates by classification level
  • Penalty rates for evenings, weekends, and public holidays
  • Overtime rates and when they trigger
  • Casual loading and how it interacts with other rates
  • Allowances for specific tasks or conditions
  • Break entitlements

Modern awards are not optional. Your employer must comply — regardless of what your contract says. A contract cannot offer less than the award minimum. If it does, the award overrides it.

How to Report Underpayment to Fair Work — A Step-by-Step Guide

Last updated: March 2026

If you've found out you've been underpaid, acting on it can feel overwhelming — especially if you're worried about your job. But you have strong legal protections, a clear process to follow, and a government agency whose entire job is recovering money on your behalf. This guide walks through every step.

If you've identified a pay shortfall and want to know what to do — start here.

Real example

Scenario: Kitchen hand discovers after 2 years they were never paid Sunday rates. Underpayment estimated: ~$4,800 across 2 years Process: Raised with employer → employer disputed → FWO complaint lodged → full recovery How long: ~4 months from complaint to resolution Key evidence: Rosters showing shift days + payslips showing flat rate

Step 1: Document everything first

Before raising anything with your employer, gather what you can:

  • Payslips — as many as possible, as far back as possible
  • Roster records — anything showing days and hours worked
  • Bank statements — show payment dates and amounts if payslips are missing
  • Employment contract or letter of engagement
  • Any written communications about pay, hours, or classification

You don't need everything to make a complaint — but more evidence means a stronger case.

Step 2: Raise it with your employer

This is usually the right first step. Many underpayments aren't intentional. A direct, professional conversation often resolves it quickly.

How to approach it:

  • State the issue factually: "I've checked the award and I believe my Sunday rate has been applied incorrectly."
  • Refer to the award and pay guide by name and clause
  • Give them a week to respond — and follow up in writing

If they acknowledge the error and fix it, you're done. If they dispute, deny, or become hostile — move to Step 3.

Step 3: Lodge a complaint with the Fair Work Ombudsman

The Fair Work Ombudsman (FWO) is the government body responsible for investigating workplace underpayment. Their service is free.

How to lodge:

  • Online: fairwork.gov.au/contact-us
  • Phone: 13 13 94 (Monday to Friday, 8am–5:30pm)

What to include:

  • Your name and employer details
  • The award you believe applies
  • The nature and dates of the underpayment
  • Any evidence gathered

What the FWO can do:

  • Investigate your employer's records
  • Issue compliance notices
  • Recover unpaid wages and interest going back 6 years
  • Refer serious cases for prosecution

If you've identified a shortfall but haven't calculated the exact amount, do that first. → [Check my pay →] A specific number makes your complaint much stronger.

Step 4: Know your protections

You cannot be legally dismissed, demoted, or disadvantaged for making a Fair Work complaint. This is called the "general protections" provision. If your employer takes adverse action because you raised a pay complaint, that is a separate and serious legal breach — and you can make a further complaint about it.

Wage Theft in Australia — How Widespread Is It?

Last updated: March 2026

Wage theft is not a niche problem in Australia. It is endemic. The Fair Work Ombudsman recovers over half a billion dollars in underpaid wages every year — and research consistently shows that represents a fraction of the total. The scale of wage theft in Australia

  • The Fair Work Ombudsman recovers $500m+ in underpaid wages annually
  • Independent research suggests the true figure across Australian workplaces is several billion dollars per year
  • Hospitality, retail, and fast food consistently top the list of most investigated industries
  • Workers aged 15–24 are the most likely to be underpaid — and the least likely to report it

These figures represent real money that should be in workers' pockets.

Is it always deliberate?

No. Underpayment falls into three categories:

Deliberate — knowing underpayment to reduce costs. Flat rates designed to absorb penalties, keeping workers on wrong classification levels.

Systemic payroll errors — incorrect award interpretation, misconfigured payroll software. Often affects large numbers simultaneously.

Ignorance — small employers who don't understand the award system.

All three result in underpayment. The worker's entitlement is the same regardless of whether the cause was intentional.

The new criminal wage theft laws

From 1 January 2025, deliberate wage theft is a criminal offence under federal law:

  • Fines of up to 3× the amount underpaid (or $7.8m for corporations, whichever is higher)
  • For individuals: up to 10 years imprisonment in the most serious cases

This applies to intentional underpayment. The threshold for "intentional" has widened significantly, and the consequences for employers who routinely underpay are now severe.

Flat Rate vs Award Pay — Is Your Flat Rate Legal?

Last updated: March 2026

If your employer pays you one rate every shift regardless of the day, there's a high chance you're being underpaid. Flat rates are legal in Australia — but only under very specific conditions that most employers don't actually meet. This page explains those conditions and how to check if yours qualifies.

If you're paid the same rate every shift no matter what day it is — this applies to you.

Real example

Scenario: Hospitality worker told their $34/hr flat rate "covers everything including weekends." The problem: Level 2 casual public holiday rate is $56.88/hr. A $34/hr flat rate doesn't come close. Underpayment on one 8-hour public holiday shift: ~$183 Why it happens: Employer picks a number above the ordinary rate and assumes it covers everything. Nobody checks the public holiday scenario.

When is a flat rate legal?

A flat rate is legal only if it is demonstrably higher than the total amount owed under the award across every possible shift scenario, including:

  • All penalty rates (Saturday, Sunday, public holiday)
  • All applicable allowances (split shift, meal, tool)
  • Overtime rates for any hours beyond the threshold

This test must be met across every single pay period — not just on average. A flat rate that looks fine on a quiet Monday-to-Friday week may fail the moment a public holiday or late-night Sunday shift occurs.

Step 3: Check penalty rates for every day type you work. → See your award's [penalty rates guide →]

Step 4: Check allowances. Does your flat rate include any applicable split shift, meal, or tool allowances? → See [award allowances explained →]

Hospitality vs Restaurant Award — Which One Covers You?

Last updated: March 2026

If you work in a café, restaurant, or hospitality venue and have never verified which award applies, there's a high chance you don't know — and there's a meaningful chance the wrong one is being used. The Hospitality Award (MA000009) and the Restaurant Industry Award (MA000119) cover similar workers in similar settings. But the rates differ, particularly on Sundays — meaning the wrong award could mean every Sunday shift has been calculated incorrectly from the start.

If you work in a café, restaurant, hotel dining room, or any food and beverage venue — this applies to you.

Real example

Scenario: Waitstaff at a standalone café. Employer applies the Hospitality Award. The Restaurant Award actually applies. Why it matters: Sunday permanent rates differ between the two awards. Restaurant Award Sunday rate (1.75×) is higher than Hospitality Award (1.5×). Impact: Potentially underpaid on every Sunday shift for the duration of employment. Why it happens: Employer uses whichever award they're familiar with. Worker has no way to check without knowing which applies.

The core distinction

The difference comes down to the employer's primary business activity.

Hospitality Award (MA000009) applies when the primary business is:

  • Hotel, motel, resort, or accommodation
  • Licensed club
  • Function centre connected to an accommodation venue
  • Pub or bar operating as part of a hotel

Restaurant Industry Award (MA000119) applies when the primary business is:

  • Standalone restaurant
  • Standalone café or coffee shop
  • Catering business not connected to a hotel
  • Event catering

If your payslip doesn't show which award applies, that's a red flag. → [Check your pay →]

1. Is your employer primarily in accommodation or hotels? → Yes → Hospitality Award

3. Is it a standalone restaurant or café with no accommodation component? → Yes → Restaurant Award

4. Is your restaurant or café attached to a hotel or resort? → Yes → Hospitality Award
